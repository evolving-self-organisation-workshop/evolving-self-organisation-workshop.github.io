Workshop description

Recent dramatic increases in the scale of deep learning infrastructures, enabled by the introduction of architectures such as the Transformer and Graph Neural Networks, have endowed them with unprecedented adaptability and enabled their application in scientific problems as complex as predicting protein structure ~\citep{abramson_accurate_2024} and forecasting global climate shifts~\citep{lam_graphcast_2023}. Yet AI systems remain brittle to disturbances~\citep{su_one_2019,qu_minimalistic_2020} and require alarming amounts of human, energy and financial resources~\citep{strubell_energy_2019}.

Biological organisms, on the other hand, solve complex problems like growing brains from a handful of cells and regenerating limbs, through \textit{self-organisation}: collectives of simple components interact locally with each other to give rise to macroscopic worlds in the absence of centralised control. Shaped by evolution, these self-organisation processes are highly energy-efficient, adaptive and robust to failures, as the redundancy built in the collective enables repurposing components, crucially, by leveraging the same self-organisation process that created the system in the first place. Can we improve AI systems by leveraging such principles? And can such nature-inspired AI systems prove useful tools for science and applications? Our workshop aims at discussing these questions at NeurIPS by bringing together experts from biology, neuroscience, complex systems, robotics and AI, inviting research at the intersection of deep learning and self-organisation.

Ideas from self-organisation have been at the core of AI since its inception. In 1986 Minsky stated in his book the Society of Mind~\cite{Minsky88} that “intelligence is an emergent property of interactions between simpler parts, that, if you dig deep enough, are not themselves intelligent at all”. Ideas from neuroscience and biology have been impregnating AI, from connectionist models~\citep{McClelland2019ConnectionistM} to neuro-inspired plasticity mechanisms~\citep{hebb1949organization}, neuroevolution algorithms~\citep{stanley_designing_2019} and indirect encodings inspired by the genomic bottleneck found in nature~\citep{zador_critique_2019, ha_hypernetworks_2016, najarro_towards_2023}. Cellular Automata~\citep{vonNeumann1966} were inspired from the process of morphogenesis and, with their more recent extension to Neural Cellular, have shown promising results in pattern formation in high dimensional spaces such as images~\citep{mordvintsev2020growing} and functional machines in Minecraft. This line of works has shown that such representations can improve generalisation and significantly reduce the size of the optimisation space.

Speakers

Antony Zador

Cold Spring Harbor Laboratory

Tony is an Alle Davis and Maxine Harrison Professor of Neurosciences . He studies how circuitry in the brain gives rise to complex behaviors,

Robin Hiesinger

Freie Universität Berlin

Robin is a professor of Neurogenetics at the Institute of Biology of the Freie Universität Berlin. He is the author of the book "The self-assembling brain".

Jascha Achterberg

University of Cambridge

Jascha recently graduated from his PhD at the MRC Cognition and Brain Sciences Unit, University of Cambridge and is about to join Rui Ponte Costa's lab as a postdoctoral research fellow. He studies the general principle underlying computations in biological and artificial intelligence, combining large scale neuroscience data with artificial neural networks and hardware-optimisation to work out which features underlie highly efficient learning and inference in brains to translate them into neuroscience-inspired artificial intelligence (NeuroAI). With this I hope to understand the general principles underlying computation in biological and artificial networks.

Iain Couzin

Max Planck Institute of Animal Behavior

ain Couzin is Director of the Department of Collective Behavior (Max Planck Institute of Animal Behavior) and a Full Professor at the University of Konstanz, where he is also a spokesperson of the Cluster of Excellence ‘Centre for the Advanced Study of Collective Behaviour’. Previously he was a Full Professor in the Department of Ecology and Evolutionary Biology at Princeton University (2013), and prior to that a Royal Society University Research Fellow in the Department of Zoology, University of Oxford, and a Junior Research Fellow in the Sciences at Balliol College, Oxford (2002-2007). His work aims to reveal the fundamental principles that underlie evolved collective behavior, and consequently his research includes the study of a wide range of biological systems, from insect swarms to fish schools and primate groups. In recognition of his research he has been recipient of the Searle Scholar Award in 2008; top 5 most cited papers of the decade in animal behavior research 1999-2010; the Mohammed Dahleh Award in 2009; Popular Science’s “Brilliant 10” Award in 2010; National Geographic Emerging Explorer Award in 2012; the Scientific Medal of the Zoological Society of London in 2013; a Web of Science Global Highly Cited Researcher in all years from 2018 to 2022; the Lagrange Prize “the first, and most important, international recognition in the field of complexity science” in 2019; and Germany’s highest research honor, the Leibniz Prize, in 2022.

Sabine Hauert

University of Bristol

Sabine is Associate Professor (Reader) of Swarm Engineering at the University of Bristol in the UK. Her research focusses on making swarms for people, and across scales, from nanorobots for cancer treatment, to larger robots for environmental monitoring, or logistics. Before joining the University of Bristol, Sabine engineered swarms of nanoparticles for cancer treatment at MIT, and deployed swarms of flying robots at EPFL. Sabine is also President and Co-founder of Robohub.org, and executive trustee of AIhub.org, two non-profits dedicated to connecting the robotics and AI communities to the public.

Ida Momennejad

Microsoft Research NYC

I'm a Principal Researcher at Microsoft Research NYC. In this role, I broadly focus on building and evaluating generative AI, inspired by my research in cognitive neuroscience, reinforcement learning, and NeuroAI. Specifically, I study how humans and AI build models of the world and use them in memory, exploration, & planning. I build and test brain & behavior inspired algorithms for learning & reasoning, e.g., AI for gaming with Xbox. My approach combines reinforcement learning, neural networks, large language models, & machine learning with behavioral experiments, fMRI, & electrophysiology
